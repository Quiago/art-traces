The Yoruba religion was already a syncretic religion in Africa, with influences from Islam, Catholicism, and other traditions, before many of its followers were displaced from the shores of West Africa and brought as slaves to Cuba. The original practitioners on the island, which was then under Spanish rule, incorporated elements of Catholicism both to veil their rituals and to ensure the survival of these traditional beliefs. Catholic saints were chosen and associated with individual Orishas (gods), and the attire from the Spanish colonial period still blends with Yoruba origins in today's colorful ceremonies. Santería (Way of the Saints) includes complex systems of cosmology, philosophy, initiation, priesthood, divination, sacrifice, a repertoire of praise songs, drums, and dance, to name a few. Santería priests who attend to the Orishas can be men or women and are called Santeros. On the other hand, the Babalaw is always male and is the one who performs divinations, mainly using a system of 16 cowrie shells.
